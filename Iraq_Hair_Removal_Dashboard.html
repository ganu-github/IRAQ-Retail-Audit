Sharp Insights — Strategic View (using K Units / packs as the comparable basis)

1. Halawa is the true volume leader — not Wax Strips
By consumer packs, Halawa (Paste + Powder) is 52.3% of the category, the largest segment by transactions. This was completely hidden in the Nielsen KGs view (which made Wax Strips look like 89%). The KGs distortion comes from how Nielsen reports pack weight for strip products. Wax Strips are actually only 4.0% of category by units, despite VEET's apparent dominance.
2. FEM is a top-3 player in the category
Restated on units: FEM holds 16.9% category volume share and 27.9% value share — far more substantial than the misleading "4.2% by KGs" headline. FEM is the clear leader in Depilatory Creams (57.5% volume / 56.1% value), the segment with the highest absolute value per pack.
3. Volume vs Value — segments tell different stories
By units, the rank is Halawa > Creams > Spray. By value, the rank is Creams (49.3%) > Halawa (22.0%) > Spray (15.5%) > Wax Strips (8.7%). Avg price/pack: Wax Strips 4,511 IQD vs Creams 3,518 IQD vs Halawa 874 IQD. Creams command the highest pack price among major segments.
4. Channel routing remains segment-specific
Modern, branded segments — Creams, Spray, Lotion — flow primarily through Perfumery. Traditional, local segments — Halawa and Bulk Wax — concentrate in Traditional Trade. Wax Strips also flow through Perfumery. This implies different go-to-market motions: shelf negotiation vs. distributor depth.
5. Each segment has a clear leader
Five of six segments are dominated by one brand: VEET in Wax Strips (96%), FEM in Creams (57%), J. Casanova in Spray (78%), J. Casanova in Lotion (95%), Soft Skin Sada Alkalej in Halawa (39%, more fragmented), and LIN Almutahida in Bulk Wax (61%, most fragmented).
6. Promotional activity is muted
Promo penetration is under 3% of pack volume in every segment. Creams have the highest promo intensity at 2.8%, Wax Strips at 1.4%, the rest minimal. Limited deal pressure suggests pricing power for established brands and an opening for promo-led entry.
7. White space for FEM (Dabur)
FEM is absent from 4 of 6 segments: Halawa (52.3% of category packs — the biggest miss), Spray (9.4%), Bulk Wax, and Lotion & Liquid. The Halawa segment is the largest white-space opportunity by transactions, dominated by Saudi/Iraqi heritage brands. Spray is the most attractive premium adjacent move — high pack price (3,433 IQD), single foreign incumbent (J. Casanova), runs through Perfumery where FEM already wins.
8. Brand fragmentation index
Brand counts vary 7x — from 5 brands in Wax Strips (consolidated) to 35 in Creams and 34 in Halawa (long tail). Fragmented segments offer share-grab opportunity; consolidated segments require price disruption or differentiated proposition.

14. Distribution BenchmarkingTop 20 brands by Weighted Distribution

#BrandManufacturerOrigin UNIT%Vol MS (nat.) %VAL%Numeric Distrib (ND)Weighted Distrib (WD)WD vs Share Gap

Methodology: Brand-level ND and WD are computed as a probabilistic union across the brand's SKUs — WDbrand ≈ 1 − ∏(1 − WDsku/100) — under the assumption that SKU stocking is independent across stores. This sits between MAX (lower bound) and SUM (upper bound) and approximates the % of weighted stores carrying any SKU of the brand. Gap = WD − UNIT%; positive = strong distribution but underperforming offtake (room to grow rate-of-sale); negative = strong offtake on limited reach (room to expand distribution).
